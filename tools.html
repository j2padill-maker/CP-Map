Formula Reference
▶
To find amperage from a millivolt drop reading:
Amps = mV reading × pipe constant ÷ footage (riser to riser)
▶
Thin wall pipe carries less material cross-section, resulting in lower resistance and a lower constant. Always confirm wall type before selecting constant — using the wrong one will miscalculate current by 40–45% on 4" and 6" pipe.

📏CP Field Formulas
Cathodic protection formulas — NACE/AMPP standards reference
1
Wenner 4-Pin Method — Soil Resistivity
ρ = 191.5 × a × R
ρ (rho) — Soil resistivity (Ohm-cm)
a — Electrode spacing (feet) — also represents depth of investigation
R — Resistance measured by soil resistance meter (Ohms)
Standard field method for determining soil resistivity. Wider spacing = deeper investigation.
⚡ Example Calculation
Given: electrode spacing a = 5 ft, meter reads R = 3.2 Ω
ρ = 191.5 × 5 × 3.2
ρ = 191.5 × 16
ρ = 3,064 Ohm-cm
Interpretation: 3,000–10,000 Ohm-cm is considered moderately corrosive soil. Values below 1,000 Ohm-cm are highly corrosive and may require increased CP current output.
2
Anode Consumption — Faraday's Law
W = K × I × t
W — Weight loss / mass consumed
K — Electrochemical equivalent (lbs/A-yr: magnesium ≈ 17.2, zinc ≈ 23.5)
I — Current (Amperes)
t — Time (years)
Calculates theoretical mass of an anode required to provide a specific current for a set lifespan.
⚡ Example Calculation
Given: magnesium anode (K = 17.2 lbs/A-yr), current output I = 0.5 A, design life t = 10 years
W = 17.2 × 0.5 × 10
W = 17.2 × 5
W = 86 lbs of magnesium consumed
Interpretation: A standard 17 lb magnesium anode would be consumed in roughly 2 years at this current draw. You would need at least a 60 lb anode (or multiple anodes in parallel) to achieve the 10-year design life.
3
Ohm's Law
V = I × R
V — Potential difference (Volts)
I — Current (Amperes)
R — Resistance (Ohms)
Core relationship for calculating voltage drops, current flow, and circuit resistance in both ICCP and galvanic systems. See the Ohm's Law tool for the interactive solver.
⚡ Example Calculation
Given: rectifier output V = 6 V, total circuit resistance R = 1.5 Ω — find current output
I = V ÷ R
I = 6 ÷ 1.5
I = 4.0 A output current
Interpretation: If the rectifier is set to 6V and the measured current is significantly lower than 4A, circuit resistance is higher than expected — check for broken bonds, corroded connections, or increased soil resistance since last survey.
4
IR Drop — Voltage Drop Correction
Vmeasured = Vpolarized + (I × Rsoil)
Vmeasured — Total voltage read by the voltmeter (ON-read)
Vpolarized — True pipe potential vs electrolyte (instant-OFF read)
I × Rsoil — Error voltage from current flowing through soil resistance
Isolates the true polarized potential of the pipe from errors introduced by current flowing through soil resistance — the basis for instant-off readings.
⚡ Example Calculation
Given: ON-read Vmeasured = −0.980 V, current I = 2 A, soil resistance Rsoil = 0.05 Ω
IR error = 2 A × 0.05 Ω = 0.100 V (100 mV)
Vpolarized = −0.980 − 0.100
Vpolarized = −0.880 V (instant-off potential)
Interpretation: The ON-read of −980 mV appears to pass the −850 mV criterion, but 100 mV of that is IR error from soil current. The true polarized potential is −880 mV — still passing, but much closer to the threshold. This is why instant-off reads are critical for accurate NACE compliance.
5
Current Density
J = I / A
J — Current density (mA/m² or mA/ft²)
I — Total current required (mA)
A — Total surface area of structure (m² or ft²)
Determines total current required for protection based on the surface area and coating efficiency of the pipeline.
⚡ Example Calculation
Given: 500 ft of 2" bare steel pipe — surface area ≈ A = 261 ft² (π × 2/12 × 500), target current density J = 1.0 mA/ft² (typical bare steel)
I = J × A
I = 1.0 mA/ft² × 261 ft²
I = 261 mA (0.261 A) required
Interpretation: A well-coated pipe of the same length may only need 0.1–0.01 mA/ft², reducing the required current by 10–100×. Coating degradation over time directly increases current demand — rising rectifier output requirements are a key indicator of coating failure.
📋 Field Use Notes
Data Integrity: Always record the reference electrode used when documenting readings — e.g., CSE (Copper-Copper Sulfate Electrode).
Environmental Factors (San Diego): High-moisture seasons and coastal proximity lower R in the Wenner formula, requiring different current output adjustments compared to dry summer months.
